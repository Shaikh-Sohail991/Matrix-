DC Power Supply

+
1.What is a DC power supply?

A DC (Direct Current) power supply is an electronic device that converts AC mains electricity into a regulated, stable DC voltage and current output. It is used to power electronic devices, test equipment, and components that require a constant, low-noise DC source. DC power supplies are widely used in R&D labs, manufacturing, and field testing environments.

+
2.What is an adjustable power supply?

An adjustable power supply allows users to set and vary both the output voltage and current within a specified range. Unlike fixed power supplies, adjustable models provide greater flexibility for testing different devices and components with varying power requirements.

+
3.What is a programmable DC power supply?

A programmable DC power supply can be controlled and configured via software or a digital interface (such as USB, RS232, LAN, or GPIB). Users can set precise voltage and current values, create automated test sequences, and monitor output remotely — making it ideal for automated test environments (ATE).

+
4.What is a linear power supply?

A linear power supply uses a linear voltage regulator (such as a series pass transistor) to reduce and regulate the voltage from a transformer. It produces very low noise and excellent transient response, making it ideal for sensitive analog circuits and precision measurement applications.

+
5.What is a bidirectional power supply?

A bidirectional power supply can operate both as a source (supplying power to the DUT) and as a load (absorbing power from the DUT). This is especially useful for battery testing, regenerative energy applications, and motor drive simulations where energy needs to flow in both directions.

+
6.What is a switching power supply?

A switching power supply (SMPS) uses a high-frequency switching transistor to convert and regulate power. Compared to linear supplies, switching supplies offer higher efficiency, smaller size, and lighter weight, though they may produce more ripple and electromagnetic interference (EMI).

+
7.Can a DC power supply simulate a battery?

Yes. Many programmable DC power supplies support battery simulation mode. By programming a specific voltage, internal resistance, and capacity curve, the power supply can mimic the behavior of a battery under load — useful for testing battery-powered devices without using real batteries.

+
8.What is the difference between CV and CC mode?

CV (Constant Voltage) mode maintains a fixed output voltage regardless of load current changes. CC (Constant Current) mode maintains a fixed output current regardless of load voltage changes. The supply automatically switches between modes depending on whether the load resistance causes voltage or current to hit the programmed limit first.

+
9.What is the difference between a DC power supply and an AC power supply?

A DC power supply outputs a fixed, steady-polarity voltage, while an AC power supply outputs an alternating voltage that periodically reverses polarity. DC supplies are used for powering electronics, while AC supplies are used for testing equipment that runs on mains AC power or for simulating AC grid conditions.

+
10.Why are ripple and noise in a DC power supply important?

Ripple and noise refer to small AC fluctuations superimposed on the DC output. High ripple/noise can interfere with sensitive electronic circuits, introduce measurement errors, and cause instability in precision analog circuits. Low ripple is especially critical for audio equipment, RF circuits, and precision measurement instruments.

+
11.What key parameters should be considered when selecting a DC power supply?

Key parameters include: output voltage range, output current range, power rating (watts), ripple and noise, load regulation, line regulation, transient response, number of channels, communication interfaces (USB/LAN/RS232/GPIB), protection features (OVP, OCP, OTP), and display/readback accuracy.

+
12.What is the function of remote sense in a power supply?

Remote sensing allows the power supply to monitor the voltage directly at the load terminals rather than at its output terminals. This compensates for voltage drops caused by resistance in the connecting wires, ensuring the load receives the precise set voltage regardless of cable length or resistance.

+
13.What causes noise, and how to reduce or eliminate noise from the power supply to the DUT?

Noise can originate from switching circuits inside the power supply, ground loops, or electromagnetic interference. To reduce it: use a linear power supply for noise-sensitive applications, add external LC filters, use proper shielded cables, maintain a solid ground connection, and keep power supply cables short and away from signal cables.

+
14.If an ohmmeter is unavailable, is there a simple way to determine if a power supply has voltage output?

Yes. Connect a known resistive load (such as a light bulb or resistor of known value) to the output terminals. If the load lights up or heats up, the supply has voltage output. Alternatively, use the power supply's built-in display or digital readback function to verify the output voltage and current values.

+
15.How to achieve negative voltage supply using a DC power supply?

To achieve a negative voltage, reverse the polarity of the output connections: connect the negative terminal to the circuit's reference (common) ground and the positive terminal to the circuit ground. Some supplies support bipolar output natively. For dual-rail supplies, the two channels can be configured in series to provide positive and negative rails simultaneously.

+
16.What is dual-frequency?

Dual-frequency refers to the ability of an AC power supply to output at either 50 Hz or 60 Hz. This is useful for testing products destined for different global markets where the mains frequency differs (e.g., 50 Hz in Europe/Asia, 60 Hz in North America).

+
17.What do the five-fold protections refer to?

The five-fold protections typically refer to: OVP (Over Voltage Protection), OCP (Over Current Protection), OPP (Over Power Protection), OTP (Over Temperature Protection), and SCP (Short Circuit Protection). These safeguards protect both the power supply and the DUT from damage under abnormal operating conditions.

+
18.What are the differences between switching power supplies and linear power supplies?

Switching power supplies are more efficient, lighter, and more compact, but generate higher ripple and EMI. Linear power supplies offer very low noise, excellent regulation, and fast transient response, but are less efficient and heavier. Linear types are preferred for precision analog applications; switching types for high-power, high-efficiency applications.

+
19.What serial port options are available?

MATRIX power supplies typically support RS232, USB, LAN (Ethernet), GPIB (IEEE-488), and RS485 as standard or optional communication interfaces. The available options depend on the specific model. These interfaces enable remote control, data logging, and integration with automated test systems.

20.Can the input voltage be switched?

Many MATRIX power supplies feature a universal input voltage range (e.g., 100–240 VAC), allowing them to automatically or manually adapt to different mains voltages worldwide. Some models require a physical switch to select between 110V and 220V inputs. Please refer to the specific model's datasheet for input voltage specifications.

21.Which button is the one-key lock, and what function does it lock?

The one-key lock button (often labeled "Lock" or with a padlock icon) disables all front-panel controls to prevent accidental changes during testing. When activated, adjustments to voltage, current, and other settings via the front panel are blocked. The supply continues operating at the currently set values. The lock can usually be released by pressing the same button again.

22.Can 100 groups of voltage and current be stored/recalled?

Yes. Many MATRIX programmable power supplies support storing up to 100 groups (memory locations) of voltage and current settings. These can be recalled instantly via the front panel or remote interface, allowing quick switching between different test configurations without manually re-entering values.

+
23.Why is the product described as high-precision?

MATRIX power supplies are described as high-precision because they offer tight voltage and current regulation (typically ±0.01% or better), high readback accuracy, and low noise/ripple output. This precision is essential for accurate characterization of electronic components and devices in research and production testing.

+
24.Why is the current fluctuating significantly?

Significant current fluctuation can be caused by: an unstable load (e.g., motors or switching circuits with variable current draw), loose connections, the supply operating near the CV/CC crossover point, or incorrect current limit settings. Check all connections, verify load stability, and ensure the current limit is set appropriately above the expected load current.

+
25.If the user sets the voltage to 24V, what is an appropriate overvoltage protection setting?

A common practice is to set the OVP (Overvoltage Protection) threshold approximately 5–10% above the working voltage. For a 24V setting, an OVP of 26–27V is typically appropriate. This provides a safety margin against transient voltage spikes while still protecting the DUT from dangerous overvoltage conditions.

+
26.When controlling the power supply via a PC, can the PC control it in an AC form?

DC power supplies output DC only, regardless of how they are controlled. PC control via software interfaces (USB, LAN, GPIB) changes the set voltage/current values digitally but does not change the nature of the output. If you require an AC output, an AC power source (not a DC supply) would be needed.

+
27.How does a DC power supply switch between constant current and constant voltage modes?

The transition between CV and CC mode is automatic. In CV mode, the supply maintains the set voltage; if the load resistance drops so that the current would exceed the set current limit, the supply automatically switches to CC mode, capping the current at the set value while the voltage drops below the set point.

This typically means the current limit is set to zero or the output is not connected to a load. In CV mode, current only flows when a load is connected. Check: (1) verify the current limit setting is above zero; (2) confirm the load is properly connected; (3) ensure the output is enabled (not in standby mode).

29.Can our linear power supply set a current ramp-up?

Some MATRIX linear power supply models support programmable slew rate control for voltage and current, allowing gradual ramp-up of the output. This is useful for powering sensitive devices that may be damaged by sudden inrush current. Please check the specific model's datasheet to confirm ramp functionality.

30.What is the approximate voltage rise and fall time when using external analog control?

Voltage rise and fall times under external analog control depend on the model and output capacitance. Typically, for MATRIX switching supplies, the voltage rise/fall time is in the range of a few milliseconds to tens of milliseconds for full-scale transitions. For precise values, consult the product's specifications or conduct a measurement under your specific load conditions.

31.What is the meaning of voltage and current indicators for load regulation?

Load regulation indicates how much the output voltage or current changes when the load changes from no-load to full-load conditions. It is expressed as a percentage: Load Regulation = (V_no-load − V_full-load) / V_full-load × 100%. A lower percentage indicates better regulation and a more stable output.

+
32.When choosing a communication interface, which has better stability: LAN or GPIB?

Both LAN (Ethernet) and GPIB offer reliable communication, but GPIB is traditionally favored in lab environments for its deterministic timing, low latency, and immunity to network congestion. LAN is more flexible and supports remote access over longer distances. For most modern ATE applications, LAN is preferred due to its versatility and ease of integration.

33.What test equipment do R&D users of switching power supplies need to evaluate and measure the performance of their products and components?

R&D users typically need: a programmable DC power supply (for powering the DUT), an electronic load (for load testing), an oscilloscope (for observing ripple, transients, and switching waveforms), a power analyzer or power meter (for efficiency measurement), a spectrum analyzer (for EMI/EMC testing), and an LCR meter (for component characterization).

+
34.What is leakage current in a switching power supply? How to reduce it?

Leakage current is a small current that flows through the safety capacitors (Y capacitors) from the AC line to the chassis ground. To reduce it: use lower-value Y capacitors, improve PCB layout to minimize parasitic capacitance, add common-mode chokes, and ensure proper grounding. Note that reducing Y capacitor values may increase EMI, so a balance must be struck.

+
35.What is line regulation of a switching power supply?

Line regulation measures how much the output voltage changes in response to changes in the input (line) voltage, while the load remains constant. It is expressed as: Line Regulation = ΔV_out / V_out × 100%. A good switching power supply will maintain output stability even with significant variations in input voltage.

+
36.What is ripple noise in a switching power supply? What factors affect it?

Ripple noise is a periodic AC voltage variation superimposed on the DC output, caused by the switching action of the power supply. Factors affecting it include: switching frequency, output filter capacitor size and type, PCB layout, load impedance, and the quality of the feedback control loop. Higher switching frequency and better output filtering generally reduce ripple noise.

+
37.How to reduce harmonic currents in a switching power supply?

To reduce harmonic currents: implement Power Factor Correction (PFC) circuitry, use active front-end rectification, add input filters (EMI filters with common-mode chokes), and ensure compliance with IEC 61000-3-2 standards. PFC is the most effective method, reshaping the input current waveform to closely approximate a sinusoid.

+
38.What do PWM and PFM mean in switching power supplies? What are their respective advantages and disadvantages?

PWM (Pulse Width Modulation) varies the pulse width at a fixed frequency to regulate output — it offers predictable noise spectrum and is easier to filter, but less efficient at light loads. PFM (Pulse Frequency Modulation) varies the switching frequency at a fixed pulse width — it offers higher light-load efficiency but produces a variable noise spectrum that is harder to filter.

+
39.What factors affect the EMC of a switching power supply?

Factors affecting EMC include: switching frequency and harmonics, PCB layout (trace routing, ground plane design), component placement, shielding, input/output filter design, cable routing, transformer design, and the presence of common-mode and differential-mode noise. Proper PCB design and filtering are critical to achieving EMC compliance.

+
40.What factors are related to the efficiency of a switching power supply?

Efficiency is affected by: switching losses (in MOSFETs and diodes), conduction losses (I²R in inductors and PCB traces), core losses in transformers and inductors, gate driver losses, output rectifier losses, and control circuit power consumption. Higher switching frequency generally increases switching losses but allows smaller components.

+
41.What are the possible reasons for low output voltage of a switching power supply?

Possible reasons include: overloaded output (load drawing more current than rated), faulty feedback network, damaged output capacitors, failed rectifier diodes, incorrect set voltage, poor line regulation under low input voltage conditions, or PCB solder joint issues. Check the load against rated capacity and inspect all components in the feedback loop.

42.What is load regulation of a switching power supply?

Load regulation describes the ability of a power supply to maintain its output voltage as the load current changes. It is defined as: Load Regulation (%) = (V_no-load − V_full-load) / V_rated × 100%. Superior load regulation means minimal output voltage deviation across the full load range.

43.How does altitude affect switching power supplies?

At higher altitudes, reduced air density decreases convective cooling efficiency, potentially causing the power supply to overheat. Additionally, lower air pressure reduces the dielectric strength of air, which may affect voltage isolation and creepage distance requirements. Many supplies are derated at altitudes above 2000m. Always check the product specifications for altitude ratings.

+
44.What is the dynamic load characteristic of a switching power supply? What factors is it related to?

Dynamic load characteristic refers to how the power supply responds to rapid changes in load current, including voltage overshoot, undershoot, and settling time. It is related to: output capacitance, feedback loop bandwidth, inductor value, and the rate of load change. A fast transient response and small voltage deviation indicate good dynamic performance.

+
45.What are harmonics in a switching power supply?

Harmonics are sinusoidal voltages or currents at frequencies that are integer multiples of the fundamental frequency (e.g., 100 Hz, 150 Hz, 200 Hz for a 50 Hz supply). Switching power supplies generate harmonics due to their non-linear input current waveform, which can cause problems such as heating in distribution equipment and interference with other loads on the same circuit.

46.What are the common topologies of switching power supplies?

Common topologies include: Flyback (cost-effective for low power), Forward converter, Push-Pull, Half-Bridge, Full-Bridge (suitable for high power), Buck (step-down), Boost (step-up), and Buck-Boost (step-up/step-down). The choice depends on power level, isolation requirements, efficiency targets, and cost constraints.

47.What does EMC mean in switching power supplies?

EMC (Electromagnetic Compatibility) refers to a power supply's ability to operate correctly in its electromagnetic environment without introducing unacceptable electromagnetic disturbances to other equipment. For switching power supplies, this means meeting conducted and radiated emission limits (e.g., EN 55032, FCC Part 15) and maintaining immunity to external interference (e.g., IEC 61000-4 series).

48.Why does the accuracy of my instrument not match the specifications when I turn it on after receiving it?

Accuracy specifications are typically verified after the instrument has warmed up for a specified period (usually 30–60 minutes) under stable ambient temperature conditions. Immediately after power-on, internal components have not yet reached thermal equilibrium. Allow adequate warm-up time and ensure the ambient temperature is within the specified operating range before making precision measurements.

49.What is Ramp Time?

Ramp Time is the programmable time duration over which the output voltage or current transitions from one set value to another. By programming a ramp time, users can control the rate of voltage/current change to prevent inrush current, avoid damage to sensitive devices, or simulate real-world power conditions in automated tests.

+
50.What are the differences between switching power supplies and linear power supplies? How to choose?

Switching supplies: high efficiency (80–95%), compact, wide input range, but higher noise. Linear supplies: very low noise, excellent transient response, but lower efficiency (30–60%) and heavier. Choose switching for high-power, space-constrained, or field applications; choose linear for precision analog, RF, or audio applications where noise is critical.

+
51.Precautions for DC power supply ripple testing

When measuring ripple: use a high-bandwidth oscilloscope (≥20 MHz), connect the probe directly to the output terminals with minimal lead length, use a 50Ω termination or shielded probe to reduce pickup, set AC coupling on the oscilloscope, apply a representative load during measurement, and ensure the probe ground clip is short to minimize loop area and inductance.

+
52.Explanation of DC power supply operating modes (CV/CC)

In CV (Constant Voltage) mode, the supply actively regulates the output voltage to the set value, and the current adjusts freely up to the current limit. In CC (Constant Current) mode, the supply actively regulates the current to the set limit, and voltage adjusts freely. The mode is determined automatically: CV when R_load > V_set/I_set; CC when R_load ≤ V_set/I_set.

+
53.What is the difference between line regulation and load regulation?

Line regulation measures output stability against changes in input (mains) voltage at a fixed load. Load regulation measures output stability against changes in load current at a fixed input voltage. Both are expressed as a percentage of rated output and indicate how well the supply maintains its output under different operating conditions.

+
54.What is the difference between CC mode and a constant current source?

A power supply in CC mode acts as a current-limiting device — it only maintains the set current when the load would otherwise cause the current to exceed the limit. A true constant current source maintains the set current actively for all load resistance values, including very high impedances. In practice, CC mode in a power supply has a maximum compliance voltage, beyond which it cannot maintain the set current.

+
55.Precautions for using the SENSE function?

When using remote sense: ensure the sense wires are connected to the correct terminals (S+ to load positive, S- to load negative), keep sense wire length to a minimum, use twisted pair for sense wires to reduce noise pickup, do not exceed the maximum sense compensation voltage specified in the manual, and never connect sense wires to the wrong terminals as this can cause instability or damage.

+
56.Explanation and application precautions of four-wire output for power supplies?

Four-wire (Kelvin) output uses separate pairs of wires for current delivery and voltage sensing, eliminating the effect of wire resistance on accuracy. It is essential for precision low-resistance measurements and precise voltage delivery to sensitive loads. Ensure all four connections are secure, the sense wires are not carrying significant current, and the maximum sense compensation range is not exceeded.

+
57.Output modes of DC power supplies: Constant Voltage (CV) mode and Constant Current (CC) mode

DC power supplies operate in either CV or CC mode depending on the load. CV mode is used for most general-purpose applications (powering circuits at a fixed voltage). CC mode is used for battery charging, LED driving, and electrochemical applications where a fixed current is required. Modern supplies transition seamlessly between modes as load conditions change.

+
58.Under what conditions does a power supply activate Overvoltage Protection (OVP)?

OVP activates when the output voltage exceeds the user-programmed OVP threshold. This can happen due to: load disconnection (voltage rises above set point), feedback loop failure, external voltage being back-fed into the output terminals, or a programming error. When OVP triggers, the supply typically shuts down the output and requires manual reset or power cycling.

+
59.What do programming resolution, display resolution, and readback resolution of a programmable DC power supply refer to?

Programming resolution is the smallest increment by which a set value (voltage/current) can be changed via software command. Display resolution is the smallest value increment shown on the front-panel display. Readback resolution is the precision of the measured/returned value when queried via the remote interface. These three values may differ; readback resolution is typically used for precision measurements.

+
60.How does a power supply achieve constant voltage or constant current output?

The power supply uses a closed-loop feedback control system. In CV mode, a voltage feedback loop continuously compares the output voltage to the set reference voltage and adjusts the control element (transistor or switch duty cycle) to minimize the error. In CC mode, a current feedback loop performs the same function for current. The mode with the active feedback loop at any given moment determines the operating mode.

LCR Meter

+
1.What is an LCR meter, and what is its function?

An LCR meter is a precision test instrument used to measure the electrical properties of passive components: Inductance (L), Capacitance (C), and Resistance (R). It applies a known AC test signal to the component and measures the resulting current and voltage to calculate impedance, from which it derives L, C, R, and related parameters such as dissipation factor (D), quality factor (Q), phase angle (θ), and equivalent series resistance (ESR). LCR meters are essential in component incoming inspection, R&D characterization, and production testing.

+
2.Functions of an LCR meter and correct usage methods

An LCR meter measures impedance, inductance, capacitance, resistance, Q factor, D factor (dissipation), and ESR. Correct usage: (1) Perform open-circuit and short-circuit calibration before measurement to eliminate fixture and lead errors; (2) select the correct test frequency — component values are frequency-dependent; (3) use 4-terminal Kelvin connections for accurate low-impedance measurements; (4) set the test signal level appropriate for the component; (5) avoid touching the component during measurement to prevent body capacitance effects; (6) allow temperature stabilization for precise measurements.

+
3.What is the basic accuracy of an LCR meter? What is the actual accuracy?

Basic accuracy (or basic measurement accuracy) is the best-case impedance measurement accuracy under ideal conditions — typically specified at a standard test frequency (e.g., 1kHz), with the component at the center of the measurement range, after calibration. Typical basic accuracy is 0.05% to 0.1% for bench LCR meters. Actual accuracy in real-world use is lower due to: frequency deviation from the calibration point, component impedance near range limits, temperature effects, fixture residuals, and cable length. Always apply the full accuracy formula from the datasheet to calculate actual accuracy for your specific test conditions.

+
4.Why is four-wire measurement used in resistance testing? What is the difference from two-wire measurement?

Four-wire (Kelvin) measurement separates the current-carrying conductors from the voltage-sensing conductors. Two dedicated wires carry the test current to the component, while two separate wires measure the voltage drop directly across the component — eliminating the resistance of the test leads and contact resistance from the measurement. Two-wire measurement uses the same conductors for both current and voltage, so lead and contact resistance are included in the reading. For low-resistance components (below ~10Ω), lead resistance (typically 10–100mΩ per lead) represents a significant error, making four-wire measurement essential for accurate results.

+
5.Can an LCR meter measure non-ideal components?

Yes. LCR meters measure the complex impedance of real-world components and can represent the result using equivalent circuit models — series or parallel. A real capacitor, for example, has ESR (equivalent series resistance) and ESL (equivalent series inductance) in addition to its nominal capacitance. The LCR meter can display these parasitic elements by selecting the appropriate equivalent circuit model. This makes LCR meters very useful for characterizing non-ideal or aged components, determining self-resonant frequency, and modeling components for circuit simulation.

Withstand Voltage Tester

+
1.What is a withstand voltage tester, and in which scenarios is it mainly used

A withstand voltage tester (also called a hipot tester — High Potential tester) applies a high voltage between a product's electrical circuits and its chassis/enclosure to verify that the insulation can withstand abnormal voltages without breakdown. It is mainly used in: (1) electrical safety compliance testing (IEC, UL, CE); (2) production-line quality control for power supplies, motors, transformers, and appliances; (3) incoming material inspection of cables and insulation; (4) maintenance testing of high-voltage equipment to verify insulation integrity over time.

+
2.What is a withstand voltage test?

A withstand voltage test (dielectric strength test or hipot test) applies a specified high voltage — significantly higher than the normal operating voltage — across the insulation of a product for a defined time period. The test verifies that the insulation does not break down (arc, puncture, or allow excessive leakage current). If the insulation holds and leakage current remains below a specified threshold throughout the test duration, the product passes. This test is a fundamental requirement in virtually all electrical safety standards worldwide.

+
3.What is the capacity of a withstand voltage tester, and how is it calculated?

The capacity of a withstand voltage tester refers to its maximum output current (or VA rating) at the test voltage. It is calculated as: Capacity (VA) = Test Voltage (V) × Maximum Output Current (A). For example, a tester rated at 5kV/100mA has a capacity of 500VA. The required capacity depends on the capacitance of the device under test — highly capacitive loads (long cables, large transformers) draw significant charging current. Ensure the tester's current rating exceeds the maximum expected leakage and charging current to avoid tripping the tester's protection circuit during a valid test.

+
4.How to determine the test voltage used when performing a withstand voltage test on a product?

The test voltage is determined by the applicable safety standard for the product category (e.g., IEC 60950, IEC 62368, UL 60601). Generally, the test voltage is set to: 2× the rated working voltage + 1000V for AC hipot, or 1.414× that value for DC hipot. For example, a product with 250V AC working voltage typically requires a 1500V AC hipot test. Always refer to the specific standard applicable to your product rather than using a general formula, as requirements vary by insulation class, pollution degree, and overvoltage category.

+
5.How to decide whether to use AC or DC withstand voltage test when performing a withstand voltage test on a product?

AC hipot is the default test method specified by most safety standards and more closely simulates real-world insulation stress. DC hipot is used when: (1) the product has high capacitance (cables, filters) that would draw excessive AC charging current; (2) the standard permits DC as an alternative; (3) testing is done in the field on installed equipment. Note that DC stress is not identical to AC stress — DC charges the insulation differently (polarization effects), so DC test levels are typically set higher (×1.414) to provide equivalent stress to AC. Always check the applicable standard for guidance.

+
6.What is the difference between AC withstand voltage test and DC withstand voltage test?

AC hipot applies a sinusoidal high voltage that stresses insulation both polarities every cycle, revealing weaknesses from partial discharge and ion migration. It draws capacitive charging current which can cause false trips on highly capacitive loads. DC hipot applies a unidirectional high voltage — it draws virtually no steady-state capacitive current, making it easier to detect true leakage current accurately. DC is gentler on insulation (no repeated polarity reversals) but charges the insulation asymmetrically. Both methods can detect breakdown; AC is standard for production testing while DC is preferred for field testing of long cables and installed systems.

+
7.What is an Insulation Resistance (IR) test?

An Insulation Resistance test measures the DC resistance of insulation material by applying a known DC voltage (typically 500V or 1000V) and measuring the resulting leakage current. The insulation resistance is calculated as R = V/I and is expressed in MΩ or GΩ. Higher IR values indicate better insulation quality. IR testing is used to detect moisture ingress, contamination, physical damage, or aging of insulation that reduces its resistance before it reaches a catastrophic failure point detectable by hipot testing. Good insulation typically measures hundreds of MΩ to several GΩ.

+
8.What is the difference between a withstand voltage test and an insulation resistance test?

The hipot (withstand voltage) test applies a high overvoltage stress to verify insulation can survive abnormal voltage events — it is a pass/fail destructive-style test used to confirm insulation adequacy. The insulation resistance test applies a low DC voltage to measure the resistance of insulation — it is a diagnostic/quantitative test that reveals the condition of insulation over time. Hipot testing stresses the insulation harder and is more likely to age or damage it slightly if repeated excessively. IR testing is gentler and can be performed more frequently as part of preventive maintenance.

+
9.What is Continuity Check?

A continuity check (also called ground continuity or protective earth continuity test) verifies that there is a low-resistance electrical connection between the ground pin of the AC input and all exposed metal parts of the equipment. This ensures that in the event of an internal fault, the fault current will flow through the ground conductor rather than through a person touching the equipment. A continuity check typically passes at resistance values below 0.1Ω to 0.3Ω (per applicable standards). It is a mandatory safety test for Class I electrical equipment.

+
10.What is a Ground Bond test?

A Ground Bond test (also called a ground continuity test or PE bond test) is a more stringent version of the continuity check. It passes a high current (typically 10A to 30A AC) through the ground path and measures the voltage drop to calculate resistance. By using high current, it stresses the ground connection and verifies it can carry fault currents safely without the connection failing. It is specified in safety standards such as IEC 60950, IEC 62368, and UL 60601 as a required production test for Class I equipment to ensure reliable protective earth bonding.

+
11.What is the difference between Continuity Check and Ground Bond test?

Both tests verify the integrity of the protective earth (ground) path, but differ in method and rigor. A continuity check uses a low test current (typically ≤200mA) with a simple resistance measurement — it confirms that a ground connection exists. A ground bond test uses a high test current (10A–30A) and measures the resulting resistance under load — it verifies that the ground connection can safely carry substantial fault currents without failure. Ground bond testing is more sensitive to poor crimps, loose terminations, and high-contact-resistance joints that a simple continuity check might pass.

+
12.What is an arc?

In the context of hipot testing, an arc is a sudden electrical discharge through or across an insulating medium when the electric field exceeds the dielectric strength of the material. Arcing produces a bright flash, intense heat, and a characteristic current spike. During a hipot test, the tester's arc detection circuit monitors for rapid current spikes (typically >1mA change in <1ms) that indicate arcing. An arc can permanently damage insulation, so the tester immediately cuts off the test voltage upon detection and flags the test as a failure.

+
13.How to determine the test voltage for a withstand voltage test?

Test voltage is determined by: (1) identifying the applicable product safety standard (e.g., IEC 62368-1, UL 508); (2) determining the working voltage of the circuit being tested; (3) identifying the insulation type (basic, supplementary, reinforced, functional); (4) determining the overvoltage category and pollution degree. For a typical 230V AC mains-connected product with reinforced insulation per IEC 62368-1, a 3000V AC hipot test for 1 second is required. Always derive test parameters directly from the standard — do not guess.

+
14.The difference between AC withstand voltage test and DC withstand voltage test

AC withstand voltage test: applies sinusoidal AC high voltage, stresses insulation both polarities, draws capacitive charging current, is the standard production test per most safety standards, and is more sensitive to surface tracking and partial discharge. DC withstand voltage test: applies steady unidirectional high voltage, draws no capacitive steady-state current (only true leakage), is easier to set precise leakage current limits, is gentler on capacitive loads, and is preferred for field testing of cables and installed systems. The DC test voltage is typically set to 1.414× the equivalent AC test voltage to provide equivalent peak stress.

Oscilloscope

+
1.What is an oscilloscope?

An oscilloscope is an electronic test instrument that displays electrical signals as waveforms plotted against time (voltage on the Y-axis, time on the X-axis). It allows engineers to observe the shape, frequency, amplitude, rise time, and timing relationships of electrical signals in real time. Modern digital oscilloscopes (DSOs) digitize the signal, store waveform data, and provide automated measurements. Oscilloscopes are fundamental tools in electronics design, debugging, manufacturing test, and education.

+
2.What is The concept of triggering?

Triggering is the mechanism by which an oscilloscope synchronizes the start of its waveform acquisition to a specific event in the signal, producing a stable, non-drifting display. The trigger circuit watches the input signal and starts the timebase sweep when the signal crosses a defined threshold (trigger level) in a specified direction (rising or falling edge). Without triggering, repetitive waveforms appear to drift or blur. Advanced trigger types include pulse width, runt pulse, pattern, serial protocol, and video triggers for capturing specific events in complex signals.

+
3.What is the capture rate of an oscilloscope?

Waveform capture rate (or waveform update rate) is the number of complete waveform acquisitions the oscilloscope can capture and display per second, expressed in waveforms/second or frames/second. A high capture rate (e.g., 500,000 wfm/s) increases the probability of capturing rare, intermittent events such as glitches, dropouts, or runt pulses. Entry-level oscilloscopes may capture only hundreds to thousands of waveforms per second, while high-performance instruments capture millions. This is an important but often overlooked specification when debugging infrequent faults.

+
4.Why does the output not change when switching the output impedance between high impedance and 50 ohms?

The output impedance setting on a signal generator/oscilloscope output must be matched to the cable and termination. If you switch the oscilloscope input between 1MΩ (high impedance) and 50Ω but the signal source is not driving a 50Ω load, the voltage at the oscilloscope input will be the open-circuit source voltage in both cases — because the source does not change. The difference becomes apparent only when there IS a 50Ω termination at the load end: with 50Ω input selected, the scope terminates the line correctly; with 1MΩ, reflections occur. Ensure both source output impedance and scope input impedance are correctly matched.

+
5.What is the function of external triggering?

External triggering allows the oscilloscope to synchronize its acquisition to a signal applied to a dedicated external trigger input (EXT TRIG), rather than to the signal being measured on the input channels. This is useful when: (1) the trigger signal is separate from the measured signal (e.g., a sync pulse from a function generator); (2) triggering on the measured signal itself is difficult due to noise; (3) measuring the time delay between an event and a circuit's response; (4) the channel inputs are needed for other signals. External triggering provides a clean, stable trigger reference independent of the measured signals.

+
6.What is the bandwidth of an oscilloscope?

Bandwidth is the frequency at which the oscilloscope's measured amplitude is attenuated to 70.7% (-3dB) of the true signal amplitude. A 100MHz oscilloscope will display a 100MHz sine wave at approximately 70.7% of its actual amplitude. For accurate measurements, a rule of thumb is to use an oscilloscope with bandwidth at least 5× the highest frequency component in the signal of interest. For digital signals, consider the signal's rise time rather than clock frequency — bandwidth ≥ 0.35/rise time ensures accurate waveform reproduction.

+
7.What impact does insufficient sampling rate have on testing and measurement?

Insufficient sampling rate causes aliasing — a phenomenon where high-frequency signal components are incorrectly reconstructed as lower-frequency signals. The Nyquist theorem requires a sampling rate of at least 2× the highest signal frequency component. In practice, oscilloscopes require 5–10× oversampling for accurate waveform reconstruction. Aliasing can produce completely false waveforms that appear valid, leading to incorrect conclusions. Additionally, low sample rates reduce timing resolution, making rise time and pulse width measurements inaccurate. Always verify the effective sample rate at your selected timebase setting.

+
8.What is the real-time sampling rate of an oscilloscope?

Real-time sampling rate (or real-time sample rate) is the maximum number of samples the oscilloscope can acquire per second for a single-shot (non-repetitive) signal. It is expressed in MS/s (Megasamples/second) or GS/s (Gigasamples/second). This is distinct from equivalent-time sampling rate, which applies only to repetitive signals. For single-shot events such as glitches, power-on transients, or one-time faults, only the real-time sampling rate is relevant. Note that the maximum real-time sample rate may only apply to a single channel — when multiple channels are active, each channel may operate at a reduced sample rate.

+
9.Can an oscilloscope measure current?

Oscilloscopes measure voltage, not current directly. However, current can be measured indirectly using: (1) a current shunt resistor — measure the voltage across a known small resistance in series with the current path (I = V/R); (2) a current probe — a clamp-type transformer or Hall-effect probe that converts current to a proportional voltage signal for the oscilloscope. Current probes are the most convenient method and avoid breaking the circuit. AC current probes work on transformer principles; DC/AC current probes use Hall-effect sensors to measure both AC and DC currents.

+
10.When testing a 485 communication device with the probe set to 1X, communication errors occur. Why?

A probe in 1X mode has a much lower input impedance (~1MΩ in parallel with ~100pF) compared to 10X mode (~10MΩ in parallel with ~10pF). RS-485 is a differential bus sensitive to capacitive loading — the large capacitance of a 1X probe (~100pF) slows signal rise/fall times, distorts the waveform, and can exceed the bus's maximum load capacitance, causing communication errors. Switching to 10X mode reduces the probe capacitance by 10×, minimizing signal distortion and restoring correct RS-485 communication. Always use 10X probes when measuring fast digital signals.

+
11.What is the definition of the real-time bandwidth of a digital oscilloscope?

Real-time bandwidth is the maximum signal frequency a digital oscilloscope can accurately acquire and display for single-shot (non-repetitive) signals, limited by both the analog front-end bandwidth (-3dB point) and the real-time sample rate. For accurate single-shot capture, the sample rate must be sufficient (typically ≥5× the signal bandwidth). If the analog bandwidth is 200MHz but the real-time sample rate only supports 100MHz bandwidth under the Nyquist criterion, the effective real-time bandwidth is limited to the sample-rate-limited value. Always consider both analog bandwidth and sample rate together for single-shot measurements.

+
12.If an oscilloscope is labeled as 60MHz, can it be understood that it can measure up to 60MHz at most?

No — 60MHz is the -3dB bandwidth, meaning signals at 60MHz are displayed at 70.7% of their true amplitude. Signals above 60MHz are progressively more attenuated but not completely blocked. However, for accurate amplitude measurements, the signal frequency should be well below the bandwidth — typically no more than 1/3 to 1/5 of the specified bandwidth. For a 60MHz oscilloscope, reliable amplitude measurements are limited to approximately 12–20MHz. For frequencies near or above the bandwidth spec, measurements will have significant amplitude error.

+
13.What is the waveform capture rate of an oscilloscope, and what impact does it have?

Waveform capture rate is the number of complete waveform acquisitions per second. A high capture rate dramatically increases the probability of capturing rare events. With a low capture rate (e.g., 1,000 wfm/s), the oscilloscope is "blind" for a large fraction of time between acquisitions — a glitch lasting 1ns occurring once per second has a very low probability of being captured. At 1,000,000 wfm/s, the blind time is vastly reduced. High capture rate is critical for: intermittent fault detection, production testing with tight time limits, and catching protocol violations in digital communications.

14.When measuring a 10MHz sine wave with a 100MHz bandwidth oscilloscope, why are the results different when the probe is set to 1X and 10X?

In 1X mode, the probe has low attenuation but high capacitance (~100pF). At 10MHz, this capacitance creates a reactive load (Xc = 1/(2π×10M×100p) ≈ 159Ω) that loads the circuit under test, reducing the signal amplitude and distorting the waveform. In 10X mode, the probe has ~10pF capacitance (10× lower), reducing the reactive loading to ~1590Ω — much less disturbing to the circuit. The 10X probe's lower capacitance gives a more accurate representation of the actual signal. Additionally, the oscilloscope must have its channel set to match the probe attenuation (1X or 10X) to display the correct voltage scale.

+
15.How to measure mains electricity with a digital oscilloscope?

WARNING: Measuring mains voltage directly is hazardous. Safe methods include: (1) use a differential high-voltage probe rated for mains voltage — never use a standard single-ended probe directly on mains; (2) use an isolation transformer to float the mains circuit and reduce shock risk; (3) use a voltage divider probe or specialized mains measurement adapter. Never connect the oscilloscope ground (which is earth-referenced) directly to the live or neutral conductor — this creates a short circuit through the safety ground. Always follow local electrical safety regulations and use appropriate personal protective equipment.

+
16.Why is it inaccurate to measure high-frequency signals when the probe is in 1X mode? How should it be measured?

In 1X mode, the probe tip capacitance is ~100pF, which creates significant capacitive loading at high frequencies — attenuating the signal amplitude and distorting waveform edges. The probe's bandwidth in 1X mode is also typically limited to 5–10MHz. For accurate high-frequency measurements: (1) use 10X mode — bandwidth extends to the probe's rated bandwidth (e.g., 300MHz) and capacitance drops to ~10–15pF; (2) use an active probe for frequencies above 500MHz — active probes have input capacitances as low as 0.5–1pF; (3) keep the ground lead as short as possible to reduce lead inductance.

+
17.How to measure power supply ripple with an oscilloscope?

To accurately measure power supply ripple: (1) set the oscilloscope to AC coupling to block the DC offset and zoom in on the small AC ripple; (2) use a short ground lead or a spring-tip ground adapter — long ground leads add inductance that introduces high-frequency ringing unrelated to actual ripple; (3) connect the probe tip and ground directly at the power supply output capacitor terminals; (4) set the vertical scale to 10–50mV/div; (5) set bandwidth limit to 20MHz to filter high-frequency noise that is not relevant to switching ripple. Measure peak-to-peak voltage for the ripple specification.

+
18.What is floating ground testing? How to perform floating ground testing?

Floating ground testing measures a signal that is not referenced to earth ground — for example, the voltage across a current shunt in a high-side circuit, or signals in isolated power supplies. Since an oscilloscope's signal ground is earth-referenced, directly connecting to a floating circuit can create a dangerous short. Safe methods: (1) use a differential probe — it measures the difference between two points without referencing either to ground; (2) use an isolated oscilloscope or add a floating input adapter; (3) use a battery-powered oscilloscope. Never use two standard probes and the math channel subtraction function on mains-referenced circuits — the probe grounds will short circuit the floating circuit.

19.What is the difference between the oscilloscope's automatic frequency measurement and hardware frequency counter measurement?

The oscilloscope's automatic frequency measurement calculates frequency from captured waveform data — it measures the period of one or several waveform cycles visible on screen and computes frequency as 1/T. Accuracy depends on the number of cycles measured and digitization resolution. A hardware frequency counter, by contrast, counts actual signal transitions over a precise gated time window (e.g., 1 second), achieving much higher frequency accuracy (typically 7–10 digits). For frequency measurements requiring better than 0.01% accuracy, a dedicated frequency counter is more appropriate than the oscilloscope's automatic measurement.

20.What is the equivalent sampling rate of an oscilloscope?

Equivalent-time sampling (ETS) is a technique used to reconstruct high-frequency repetitive waveforms by collecting one or a few samples per trigger event across many trigger cycles, each time slightly shifting the sample timing. Over many repetitions, the waveform is built up with very high effective time resolution, enabling bandwidth far exceeding the real-time sample rate. Equivalent sampling rates of hundreds of GS/s are achievable. The critical requirement is that the signal must be perfectly repetitive — ETS cannot be used for single-shot or changing signals. It is commonly used for characterizing high-speed serial signals and clock edges.

21.Knowledge related to probes

Oscilloscope probes are critical to measurement accuracy. Key probe parameters: (1) Attenuation ratio (1X/10X/100X) — affects bandwidth and loading; (2) Bandwidth — must match or exceed oscilloscope bandwidth; (3) Input capacitance — lower is better for high-frequency signals (10X: ~10–15pF; active: ~0.5–1pF); (4) Input resistance — 1X: 1MΩ; 10X: 10MΩ; active: 1MΩ+; (5) Compensation — passive probes must be compensated to the specific oscilloscope channel using the calibration signal; (6) Ground lead length — minimize for high-frequency measurements; (7) Probe type: passive, active, differential, current, or high-voltage, depending on application.

22.What is memory depth?

Memory depth (or record length) is the number of sample points the oscilloscope can store in a single acquisition. It determines how long a time window can be captured at full sample rate: Capture time = Memory depth ÷ Sample rate. For example, with 1M points at 1GS/s, you can capture 1ms at full sample rate. Insufficient memory depth forces the oscilloscope to reduce its sample rate to cover the desired time window, degrading time resolution. Deep memory is essential for capturing long duration events (serial communication packets, system startup sequences) while maintaining high time resolution.

+
23.How long can an oscilloscope store waveforms, and what parameters does it depend on?

The capture duration at full sample rate depends on: Memory depth ÷ Sample rate. For example: 10M samples ÷ 1GS/s = 10ms. Longer captures require either: (1) deeper memory; (2) reducing the sample rate (by zooming out the timebase); or (3) using segmented memory acquisition to store many short captures without gaps. For very long-duration recording, some oscilloscopes offer roll mode or history memory that continuously overwrites old data. The practical capture duration ranges from nanoseconds (at maximum sample rate) to seconds or minutes (at reduced sample rates with maximum memory depth).

+
24.What is the rise time of an oscilloscope?

Rise time is the time required for the oscilloscope's step response to rise from 10% to 90% of its final amplitude. It is related to bandwidth by: Rise time ≈ 0.35 / Bandwidth (for a Gaussian response). For example, a 100MHz oscilloscope has a rise time of ~3.5ns. When measuring a signal's rise time, the measured rise time is the vector sum of the signal's true rise time and the oscilloscope's own rise time. To minimize measurement error, the oscilloscope rise time should be at least 3–5× faster than the signal's rise time. Use a higher-bandwidth oscilloscope for fast-edge digital signals.

+
25.What is trigger holdoff?

Trigger holdoff is a user-adjustable time period after a trigger event during which the oscilloscope ignores subsequent trigger events and will not re-arm. After the holdoff period expires, the trigger circuit re-arms and waits for the next qualifying trigger event. Holdoff is used to stabilize the display of complex or burst signals where multiple trigger conditions occur within a single pattern period — for example, a serial data burst followed by a long idle period. By setting holdoff slightly longer than the burst duration, the oscilloscope triggers reliably at the start of each burst rather than at spurious edges within it.

+
26.What is the difference between AC effective value and root mean square (RMS)?

They are the same thing. The RMS (Root Mean Square) value of an AC signal is calculated by taking the square root of the mean of the squares of instantaneous values over one complete cycle. The term "AC effective value" refers to the RMS value — it is called "effective" because an AC voltage with an RMS value of Vrms delivers the same average power to a resistive load as a DC voltage of the same magnitude. For a pure sine wave, Vrms = Vpeak / √2 ≈ 0.707 × Vpeak. Oscilloscopes measure true RMS when the "True RMS" measurement function is selected, which accounts for non-sinusoidal waveforms.
